Full Spectrum vs Broad Spectrum vs CBD Isolate: What Actually Matters

April 4, 2026 10 min read

You're standing in a store, or scrolling through yet another website, and there they are. Three terms on three different bottles: full spectrum, broad spectrum, CBD isolate. The prices are all over the place. The labels all look vaguely scientific. And nobody seems to explain the difference without trying to sell you something.

We get it. When we started growing hemp on our farm in Taneytown, Maryland, even we had to cut through a mountain of confusing marketing to figure out what these terms actually mean. And we grow the stuff.

So here's the straightforward version. No sales pitch. Just what these three types of CBD are, what the current science says, and why we chose to make the kind we make. You can decide what's right for you.

What's Actually in Each Type

Full spectrum hemp extract contains the complete range of compounds from the hemp plant. CBD is the main one, but you also get minor cannabinoids like CBG, CBC, and CBN, plus terpenes (the compounds that give hemp its smell) and flavonoids. And yes, it contains trace amounts of THC. Federal law caps that at 0.3% or less. Not enough to get you high, but enough to matter. We'll explain why in a minute.

Broad spectrum is similar but with the THC removed entirely. You still get multiple cannabinoids and terpenes, just with a zero next to THC on the lab report.

CBD isolate is exactly what it sounds like. Pure CBD, 99% or higher, with everything else stripped away. No other cannabinoids, no terpenes, no THC. It's the most processed form you can buy.

We think of it this way. Full spectrum is the whole orange. Broad spectrum is the orange with certain seeds carefully picked out. Isolate is just the vitamin C tablet.

The Entourage Effect: What We Know and What We Don't

You'll see the phrase "entourage effect" on a lot of CBD websites. The basic idea is that hemp compounds work better together than any single one does alone. Like how a band sounds different from one guitar playing by itself.

There's real research behind this. A 2023 study in the European Journal of Drug Metabolism and Pharmacokinetics found that full spectrum CBD had measurably higher oral bioavailability compared to CBD isolate. We're talking 12% higher in male subjects and 21% higher in female subjects. The researchers pinpointed that even just 0.2% THC was the key driver, increasing CBD's intestinal permeability. Your body literally absorbs more CBD when that trace THC is present.

But here's where we want to be straight with you, because that matters more to us than making a sale. A 2023 review of entourage effect research found that the solid evidence is still limited. Some studies show clear synergy between compounds. Others show certain compounds can actually work against each other. Most CBD brands conveniently leave that part out.

The trend line points toward full spectrum being more effective. The science isn't settled though, not the way some marketing copy makes it sound. We believe in full spectrum because of what we've seen in the research and what thousands of customers have told us works for them. But we're farmers, not doctors, and we're not going to pretend the debate is closed when it isn't.

Who Each Type Is For

Full spectrum is for people who want the whole plant and are fine with trace THC. If you don't get drug tested and you want the formulation that current research suggests is most bioavailable, this is the direction to look. It's what we make at Fiddlers Green. Our organic hemp extract is full spectrum because we think the whole plant matters.

Broad spectrum makes sense if you need to avoid THC entirely. Maybe you get tested for work. Maybe you just prefer zero THC for your own reasons. No argument from us. You're still getting a range of cannabinoids and terpenes, just without the one compound that research suggests helps with absorption.

CBD isolate has its uses too. It's flavorless, which matters to some people. It's the simplest ingredient if you're adding CBD to food or drinks. And if you want a single-compound product with no surprises, isolate gives you that. You're just missing everything else the plant makes.

There's no universally "best" type. There's the best type for your situation.

How to Tell What You're Actually Buying

Every reputable CBD company, us included, should have recent lab results from an independent third party lab. Not their own internal testing. The Certificate of Analysis (COA) should show the cannabinoid profile, THC content, and test for contaminants like heavy metals and pesticides. If a company doesn't have COAs available or makes you jump through hoops to find them, that tells you something.

Check whether the COA matches the label. If a product says "full spectrum" but the COA shows only CBD with no other cannabinoids detected, somebody's not being straight with you.

Ask about extraction method. CO2 extraction is considered the cleanest approach. It doesn't leave solvent residues and preserves the plant compounds well.

Look at carrier oils and additives. Simpler is usually better. Our extracts use organic MCT coconut oil as a carrier.

And pay attention to organic certification. We're USDA certified organic, so obviously we think this matters. Hemp is a bioaccumulator, meaning it pulls whatever is in the soil right into the plant. If that soil has pesticides or heavy metals, they end up in your extract. Organic certification means the growing practices were verified by an outside agency, not just claimed on a label.

Why We Chose Full Spectrum

We could make isolate. We could strip the THC out and call it broad spectrum. Either option would probably be easier to market.

But we're seed to shelf. We grow our hemp organically on our family farm, the same land our family has farmed since 1946. We extract it ourselves in small batches. And through that entire process, the goal is to keep the plant as close to how it grew as possible. Full spectrum is the least processed form of hemp extract, and that lines up with everything else we do here.

That said, if broad spectrum or isolate is what you need, go find the best version of it you can. Read the COAs. Ask hard questions. We'd honestly rather you buy a good broad spectrum product from someone else than a bad full spectrum from anyone. Including us.

The Short Version

The difference between full spectrum, broad spectrum, and isolate comes down to how much of the original plant is still in the bottle. Full spectrum keeps everything, including trace THC. Broad spectrum removes the THC. Isolate removes everything except CBD.

Current research leans toward full spectrum for better absorption. But the science is still developing, and we'd rather tell you that than pretend we have all the answers.

If you want to try a USDA organic, small batch, full spectrum extract made by the people who actually grew the hemp, that's what we do.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between full spectrum and broad spectrum CBD?
Full spectrum contains all the cannabinoids, terpenes, and flavonoids from the hemp plant, including trace THC (0.3% or less). Broad spectrum has the same range of compounds but with THC completely removed. The practical difference: full spectrum includes the trace THC that research shows helps your body absorb CBD more effectively.
Will full spectrum CBD show up on a drug test?
It can, yes. Even though full spectrum CBD contains only trace THC, regular use can cause it to accumulate in your system. If you get drug tested for work, broad spectrum or isolate is the safer bet.
Is CBD isolate less effective than full spectrum?
A 2023 study found 12-21% higher absorption rates for full spectrum compared to isolate, with trace THC identified as the key factor. That said, isolate still contains CBD and people do find it helpful.
How do I verify if my CBD is really full spectrum?
Ask for the Certificate of Analysis from a third party lab. A real full spectrum product will show CBD as the dominant cannabinoid plus smaller amounts of CBG, CBC, CBN, and others, along with trace THC at or below 0.3%.
Why does organic certification matter for CBD?
Hemp pulls whatever is in the soil into the plant, including pesticides and heavy metals. USDA organic certification means an outside agency verified the growing practices.
