What Is Regenerative Farming (And Why Your CBD Should Come From One)

Farm & Soil April 4, 2026 11 min read

"Regenerative" is everywhere right now. It's on coffee bags, clothing labels, and about half the booths at every farmers' market from here to Portland. Everybody's using the word. Most of them can't tell you what it actually means.

We can. Not because we read it in a marketing deck, but because we live it. We’ve been farming this ground in Taneytown, Maryland since 1946. The regenerative approach came later — when we realized that working with the land instead of against it was the only way forward.

Regenerative Farming Is Not Just "Organic Plus"

To understand regenerative, it helps to see where it sits relative to what most people already know:

Conventional farming extracts. It treats soil as a medium to hold plants upright while you pour synthetic fertilizers and pesticides on top. It works in the short term. In the long term, it degrades the land.

Organic farming stops the worst damage. No synthetic pesticides, no synthetic fertilizers. But organic is mostly a list of things you don't do. You can be certified organic and still degrade your soil through tillage and monocropping.

Regenerative farming asks a different question entirely: how do we leave this ground better than we found it? It's not just about avoiding harm — it's about actively building soil biology, increasing carbon sequestration, and creating a system that gets stronger over time instead of weaker.

The Regenerative Organic Certified (ROC) standard builds on USDA Organic by adding requirements for soil health, animal welfare, and social fairness. And the USDA itself has taken notice — launching a $700 million pilot program in 2024 for regenerative practices, the largest investment of its kind.

What It Actually Looks Like on Our Farm

No-Till Fields

Tilling is the single most destructive thing you can do to soil. It rips apart fungal networks that took years to build, releases stored carbon into the atmosphere, and destroys the soil structure that holds water. We use no-till methods, which means we deal with some ambitious weeds. That's the trade-off, and we'll take it every time.

Ground That’s Never Bare

Bare soil is dying soil. After every harvest, cover crops go in — clover, rye, vetch — that protect the ground, feed the microbiology, and fix nitrogen naturally. There are living roots in our ground year-round. That's not an accident. It's the entire point.

Rotation Over Repetition

Hemp never goes in the same field two years running. It rotates with heritage grain and cover crops in a planned sequence that keeps the soil biology diverse and prevents the pest and disease cycles that monocropping creates.

Everything Connected

The farm isn’t just hemp. It’s hemp, wheat, bees, and cover crops on the same land — a system where each piece supports the others. The bees pollinate the cover crops. The cover crops feed the soil. The soil grows the hemp. No pesticides. No fungicides. The system handles it.

What the Science Says

The research on regenerative practices has grown substantially:

  • Carbon sequestration: Regenerative agriculture has the potential to pull billions of tons of CO2 from the atmosphere. Soil is the largest terrestrial carbon sink we have, and regenerative practices are among the most effective ways to put carbon back into it.
  • Water quality: Studies show significantly less nitrate runoff from regenerative fields compared to conventional ones. That matters if you live downstream — and everyone lives downstream.
  • Drought and flood resilience: Healthy soil acts like a sponge. Regenerative fields absorb and hold water better, performing measurably better in both drought and heavy rain events.
  • Yield: Multi-year studies have found measurable yield increases from regenerative practices. The myth that regenerative means lower production doesn’t hold up in the long-term data.

The Economics Are Better Than You Think

The most common pushback on regenerative farming is that it costs more. Here's what the numbers actually show:

Significantly higher long-term profits. A Rodale Institute study comparing regenerative organic and conventional systems over 30+ years found that regenerative organic was more profitable — not marginally, but significantly — primarily due to lower input costs.

Lower input costs. When your soil biology is doing the work, you're not buying synthetic fertilizers, pesticides, or fungicides. Those savings compound year over year.

Market demand for transparency. Consumers increasingly want to know where their products come from and how they were made. Regenerative practices are a real differentiator, not because they're trendy, but because they represent a genuinely different way of farming.

The transition years are hard. We won't sugarcoat that. Moving from conventional to regenerative means a period where your soil is recovering and your yields may dip. But the economics shift permanently once the biology is established.

Why This Matters for Your CBD

Hemp is a bioaccumulator. Whatever is in the soil ends up in the plant. That's why organic certification matters — and why the soil itself matters even more.

Plants grown in biologically active soil produce more consistently. We’ve noticed the difference in our fields — healthier soil, healthier plants. That’s not a clinical claim, just what we see season after season.

Most CBD on the market comes from industrial monocrop hemp operations. Nothing wrong with that if it's grown cleanly. But we think the soil is the whole point. The plant can only be as good as the ground it grows in.

How to Know If Your CBD Comes From a Regenerative Farm

  • Ask about the farm. Can they name it? Can they show you pictures? Do they know what else grows there?
  • Look for soil health language beyond just "organic." Cover cropping, no-till, crop rotation, biodiversity — these are the practices, not just buzzwords.
  • Look for diversified operations. A farm growing only hemp is a monocrop, even if it's organic. Regenerative farms grow multiple things by design.
  • Check for transparency. Lab results, farming practices, extraction methods — if a company won't share these, ask yourself why.
  • Look for "seed to shelf." When the same people grow the hemp and make the product, there's accountability at every step. That’s what this land asks of us, and what your purchase makes possible.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is regenerative farming in simple terms?

It's a farming system designed to improve the land over time rather than just use it. Key practices include cover cropping, crop rotation, reduced or no tillage, and building biodiversity. The goal is soil that gets healthier every year.

How is regenerative farming different from organic?

Organic prohibits synthetic chemicals, which is important. But regenerative goes further — it actively rebuilds soil biology and sequesters carbon. You can be certified organic and still degrade your soil through tillage and monocropping. Regenerative addresses the health of the whole system.

Does regenerative farming affect CBD quality?

Healthier soil grows healthier plants with more consistent flower quality. The principle that what’s in the soil ends up in the plant applies directly to hemp. We’ve noticed the difference in our regenerative fields season after season.

Is regenerative farming more expensive?

Transition costs are real — there's a period where the soil is recovering. But long-term profits are significantly higher according to multi-decade studies, primarily because input costs (fertilizers, pesticides) drop dramatically when the soil biology is doing the work.

How can I tell if a CBD company uses regenerative farming?

Ask directly. Real regenerative operations can name their farm, describe their specific practices, and show you what else grows on their land. If the answer is vague or they redirect to "organic" certification alone, that tells you something.
